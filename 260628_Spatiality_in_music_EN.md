# Notes Toward a Taxonomy of Spatiality in Music

## 1.

Speaking of his piano and orchestra work *Arc* (1963–66/76), Tōru Takemitsu refers to his own drawing of a "garden" as expressing his ideas about "musical form and duration" — that is, about the temporal unfolding of music.

> Sometimes I trace a particular garden plan musically. (Takemitsu 1987)

Gordon Cullen, architect and landscape designer, proposed a way of re-reading urban space by attending to the shifts in viewpoint and scenery experienced while moving through it. He called the resulting sequence — in which varied visual stimuli appear and disappear — "Serial Vision." The following remark by Takemitsu seems close to Cullen's concept:

> Imagine a garden of a certain shape, and someone walking through it. (Takemitsu 1987)

Takashi Funayama observes that Takemitsu is clearly referring not to a Western geometrical garden but to the Japanese stroll garden (*kaiyūshiki teien*) — though gardens designed to be enjoyed through a walking tour are not exclusively Japanese; the term *kaiyūshiki teien* as a landscape term refers primarily to Japanese gardens — and goes on to write as follows. This is likely due to the trends in orchestral writing prevalent at the time *Arc* was composed—such as micropolyphony.

> Yet I think it would be virtually impossible, on listening to *Arc*, to conjure images of one scene or another from a Japanese garden, let alone to conceive of the solo pianist as a person strolling through that garden. (Funayama 1998, p. 174)

Funayama acknowledges that *Arc* is not a direct auditory depiction of a garden, while arguing that the poetics of the garden correspond to Takemitsu's musical space. Peter Burt (2001) raises a skeptical note about this kind of explanatory correspondence itself:

> Unfortunately, however, scholars have tended uniformly to explain Takemitsu's [garden] metaphor as if it were being translated into sound. (p. 126, re-translated from the Japanese edition)

He then reframes the substance of Takemitsu's "garden" metaphor through the four garden elements — Sand (S), Grass (G), Tree (T), and Rock (R) — each operating on a different temporal cycle, proceeding simultaneously:

> It seems clear that Takemitsu's use of the elements of a Japanese garden as a metaphor was intended, at least in *Arc*, to establish in the music various kinds of spatial and textural differentiation, producing a "pan-focus" texture covering the whole, similar to *Coral Island*. Takemitsu further linked each musical element to the appropriate horticultural term by depicting it in terms of its "sound cycle." (p. 126, re-translated from the Japanese edition)

The following account by Takemitsu makes legible the reasoning behind connecting simultaneously proceeding, differentiated temporal cycles to a spatial metaphor:

> I. Grass (and flowers) is composed as a mobile form in rapid change, like a collection of indeterminate solos, placed in a hetero-cyclic time relation with slightly differing periodicities.  
> II. Tree changes less rapidly than Grass (and flowers). It is composed indeterminately as a mobile form in gradual change.  
> III. Rock does not change in itself, but alters its appearance when the viewpoint changes. It is composed determinately as a stable form — a kind of timbral variation.  
> IV. Sand (and earth) exists constantly as something sustained, or as a rest, unrelated to the overall tempo. It is the metagalaxy — the entire universe — and is carried by the strings.  
> (Takemitsu 1987)

> As I have described, in this work several different tempi are superimposed. They might be compared to the landscape flowing past a train window — the rapid change of the near view, the slow change of the distant view. The conductor's tempo, the pianist's tempo, and the tempi of the individual soloists (G and T). Several different time-streams proceed simultaneously. (Takemitsu 1987)

Up through his later work *Spirit Garden* (1994), the "garden" metaphor continued to appear in Takemitsu's output, though substantially reinterpreted each time. The present concern is not the particular form of the "garden" in each work, but rather the nature of the relationship between spatiality and music as conceived through the "garden" metaphor.


## 2.

When we speak of music, we cannot escape a vocabulary drawn from space.

> Of course, there can be no doubt that music is a temporal art. (…) Yet anyone acquainted with the philosophical and theoretical literature on music will be struck by how frequently spatial terms and categories appear. Indeed, it seems impossible to speak of music without recourse to some spatial concept. (R. P. Morgan 1980)

We cannot say that pitch "height" measures so many metres, that dynamic "size" measures so many metres, or that duration "length" measures so many metres. To express phenomena lacking physical extension — pitch, dynamics, duration — we borrow words that denote physical extension: spatial vocabulary. Pitch, approximately equivalent to frequency, is the reciprocal of wavelength and therefore not entirely unrelated to the spatial dimension. Yet a wave that is "short" in wavelength is "high" in frequency, and the two are not immediately connected by ordinary linguistic intuition. Moreover, despite indicating the number of vibrations per unit time, it is rare — at least in Japanese — to describe frequency as "many."

For pitch height and duration length, the influence of Western music notation (and its descendant the frequency spectrogram) is also conceivable. High pitches are written in high positions on the staff, and proportional notation translates duration into physical distance on the page. Hence a high pitch is described as "high," and so on.

Yet the verticality metaphor is not the only way of expressing pitch. In Persian, Turkish, and Zapotec, the vocabulary most frequently used for high pitches corresponds to the English "thin," and for low pitches to "thick" (Shayan, Ozturk, and Sicoli 2011). Other vocabulary used includes small/weak, tranquil, and mild for high pitches, and strong/big and tall/high for low pitches — among which are terms carrying no spatial implication.

Occasionally there exist dedicated terms meaning specifically "high-pitched" or "low-pitched," though these include borrowings from music theory. The verticality metaphor does not hold priority in expressing pitch in these languages; yet it is worth noting that even in these languages one finds what might be called "crossmodal correspondence" — the phenomenon of linking one sense to express another.

I have just written "crossmodal correspondence," but it must be borne in mind that crossmodality remains an unresolved problem in science. With that caveat, what reason is there for using vocabulary of a different dimension — such as the distance between two points — rather than generating vocabulary that directly expresses pitch height?


## 3.

It appears that no language possesses an independent vocabulary for pitch height. Counterexamples are welcome.

Japanese and English use the spatial-verticality terms "high/low" (*takai/hikui*) for pitch. Persian, Turkish, and Zapotec use, as noted, "thin/thick" — and ancient Greek "sharp/heavy" (*oxys/barys*) — both tactile terms. Vedic Sanskrit uses terms related to bodily movement: "raised/unraised" (*udātta/anudātta*). The four tone names in Middle Chinese (level, rising, departing, entering: *píng, shǎng, qù, rù*) describe the contour, direction, and length of tones as spatial motion, though they are not expressions specific to sound. Across these languages, none possesses vocabulary native to pitch; all without exception borrow from other modalities.

As a hypothesis for why pitch height lacks its own vocabulary, I propose the conjunction (AND) of the following two conditions. When both conditions coincide, a sensory dimension fails to develop its own scalar vocabulary and instead borrows from other modalities.

1. Not visually perceptible
2. Precise scalar discrimination is not necessary for survival

Color is visually perceptible. Hence each color has its own dedicated vocabulary, though the range covered by each term varies significantly across ethnic groups and regions.

Taste and smell are not visually perceptible, but are directly relevant to survival. Hence dedicated vocabulary exists for individual tastes and smells.

Touch is said to be the most primitive sense, present even in organisms without specialized sensory organs such as eyes or ears. It is essential for detecting external threats and maintaining life. Proprioceptive vocabulary — numbness, stiffness, cramping — is naturally relevant to survival as well.

*Urusai* ("noisy") might appear at first glance to be vocabulary native to sound in modern Japanese, but the classical form *urusashi* means "troublesome" or "persistent," and is not necessarily specific to sound. Similarly, the etymological root of "noise" is the Latin *nausea*, meaning seasickness, disgust, or discomfort — a word expressing a situation rather than a sound.

Pitch height cannot be seen, nor is it directly relevant to one's own survival. From the perspective of species preservation, one might suppose that identifying a child's unusually high-pitched cry of distress could be biologically necessary. Yet in everyday vocal communication, what is discriminated is the relative change in pitch (intonation), not absolute, precise pitch discrimination — which is unnecessary, and thus, it can be argued, independent vocabulary never developed.


## 4.

Up to this point, the first question has been: "Why is spatial vocabulary chosen to express pitch?" It has become clear, however, that spatial vocabulary is not a universal choice across all languages, but is a language- and culture-dependent phenomenon. The second question — "How is spatiality in music experienced?" — must be treated as separate from the first, because the use of spatial vocabulary to express musical parameters such as pitch does not necessarily entail the experience of spatiality in music.

Those of us accustomed to the "height" metaphor for pitch — including the author — might do well to consider other languages. As noted above, there exist languages whose speakers also use vocabulary equivalent to "strong/weak" to express pitch. For speakers of such languages, the possibility that they experience music as a continuum of intensity rather than space cannot be entirely excluded. However, as long as we currently possess neither evidence to deny nor confirm this, we should for now keep the question of spatial vocabulary for sound and the question of the spatial experience of music separate from each other.

The experience of sonic spatiality in music is not limited to sound image localization (panning) in stereo or multi-channel contexts, nor to spatial simulation through acoustic signal processing such as reverb. If we were to enumerate a provisional taxonomy of categories of sonic spatiality in music, the following five present themselves.

### Category 1: Implemented space

Panning, multi-channel, spatial arrangement of performers, and the like. Space is implemented external to the listener.

### Category 2: Simulated space

Acoustic signal processing such as reverb, delay, and filters; binaural recording; the organ swell box; and similar techniques. Perceptually imitates and reproduces a space that does not exist.

### Category 3: Body-schema metaphor

High/low pitch, loud/soft dynamics, and the like. A "thin" metaphor in which a single image schema derived from bodily orientation and gravity is mapped onto a single musical attribute.

### Category 4: Formal/diagrammatic space

Diagrammatic and mathematical structures as handled in compositional technique and notation. The geometric structure of Cage's *Variations I & II*, the group-theoretic structure of Xenakis's *Nomos Alpha*, and the like. Space arises not from embodied experience but from operational and diagrammatic logic.

### Category 5: Imaginative/constructive space

Without physical implementation or acoustic simulation, space is constructed through the listener's active imagination and interpretive engagement.

#### 5a: Layering

A depth structure of foreground, middle ground, and background. A common convention of orchestration.

#### 5b: Depictive/programmatic space

Titles and other paratexts guide the listener's imagination toward a specific space.

#### 5c: Serial Vision space

The passage of time is experienced not as narrative but as spatial movement. In music without teleological closure, the listener moves and roams through space rather than advancing through time. Takemitsu's "garden" metaphor.

#### 5d: Phenomenological space

Quotation, otherness, and temporal/social/cultural distance open a space within experience. Mahler, Berio's *Sinfonia*, Stockhausen's *Hymnen* and *Kurzwellen*, and others.

The discussion of spatial vocabulary in the preceding sections corresponds to "Category 3: Body-schema metaphor." Categories 1 and 2 are set aside; the following sections examine Categories 4 and 5 through specific works.


## 5.

### Category 4: Formal/diagrammatic space

The logic common to the examples below is that an operational and diagrammatic spatial structure comes first, and that structure generates the music.

#### Cage, *Variations I & II* (1958/61)

Music is generated through diagrammatic operations in two-dimensional space. The performer reads and interprets the geometric relationships among points and lines scattered across overlapping transparent sheets.

#### Xenakis, *Nomos Alpha* (1966)

Group-theoretic operations serve as the compositional principle. Using the rotation group of a cube with eight vertices as material, parameters such as pitch, dynamics, and density are transformed.

#### Ikeda, *Planetary systems, or Double duet for the Outside of Time* (2022)

The path of an Eulerian circuit through a complete bipartite graph comprising two groups of six nodes each forms the structural backbone of Part I. The work takes as its concept the removal of time from music; by presenting one arbitrarily chosen path through a graph-based structure that exists outside of time, the entire space of possibilities is made to be represented. The graph is also displayed in the program note, making it possible, should the listener wish, to follow the graph visually while hearing the performance.

### Observations on formal/diagrammatic space

Ligeti identifies reversibility as the most essential property of space (Bauer 2023). The palindromic structures in Webern are the paradigmatic example. He invokes painting as a model for new music — in painting, the viewer's gaze moves freely, unrestricted in speed or direction. This is the self-evident manifestation of spatial reversibility. By contrast, the teleological time presupposed by the tonal system, or the "arrow of time" grounded in the second law of thermodynamics, is irreversible.

A problem with this category is that the spatiality of these works — in other words, their multiplicity — is reduced to a single path by the act of performance. The performer can grasp the spatial or plural nature of the structure, but the listener is shown only a single spatial cross-section. This asymmetry is structurally analogous to the problem faced by Boulez and others in mobile form (*forme mobile*).

For now, practical responses to this problem are as follows. One is to perform the work oneself, thereby achieving direct contact with the spatial structure. Another is for the composer to make the mapping rules explicit, allowing the listener to imagine the spatial structure. These might also be read as critiques of the conventional communicative structure in music — the one-way channel from performer to listener. Or a solution may be found by entirely unforeseen means.


## 6.

### Category 5: Imaginative/constructive space

Mahler's symphonies are a paradigmatic case of "5d: Phenomenological space" — the incursion of historically and socially identifiable otherness. Folk song, march, religious music, and popular music are introduced as foreign bodies within the symphonic context, and on each occasion temporal and social distance is experienced as spatiality in the form of an intrusion into space. Berio's *Sinfonia* (1968) adds further layers to the 5d situation: multiple voices superimpose other texts and musical fragments in strata upon the score of the third movement of Mahler's *Resurrection* Symphony. Stockhausen's *Kurzwellen* (1968) draws on shortwave radio whose transmission source cannot be identified. It may be regarded as an extreme case of incorporating contingent otherness — expanded to planetary scale — into a work.

#### Takemitsu, *Arc* (1963–66/76)

As described above, this work has the four elements — Sand, Grass, Tree, and Rock — proceeding simultaneously on different temporal axes, forming an imaginative space. This corresponds to "5a: Layering," and may be something inherited from Debussy, who created a sense of depth through contrasts of dynamics, textural density, and rhythmic activity (Thomas 2022). The garden drawing presented in Takemitsu's book — which may not have been directly attached to the work itself — functions as an inducement toward "5b: Depictive/programmatic space."

#### Takemitsu, *How slow the Wind* (1991)

Over a relatively brief duration of around ten minutes, the principal motif recurs quite frequently while subtly varying its character each time it appears. Whether Takemitsu himself wrote about this work in connection with the "garden" metaphor is not certain, but as an example of "5c: Serial Vision space" this work seems more apt than *Arc*. Takemitsu employs a similar structural principle in many works besides this one.

#### Boulez, *Dialogue de l'ombre double* (1985)

The work spatializes sound (Category 1) through six loudspeakers surrounding the audience, a seventh speaker representing a distant resonance, and a piano fitted with speakers; the pre-recorded clarinet part is produced using two microphone placements — near and far — with varying ratios of reverberation (Category 2). The existence of two selectable paths — a Roman numeral version and an Arabic numeral version — continues the mobile form concept found in *Domaines* and the *Troisième Sonate* (Category 4) (Luna 2025). According to Gerzso, the "zigzag" appearing in the movement of sound images between loudspeakers and in the arrangement of melodic material is an idea drawn from Claudel's play *Le Soulier de satin*, which also gives the work its name (5b: Depictive/programmatic space). The extraordinary situation of the pre-recorded clarinet part — which must be recorded by the concert performer themselves — a dialogue with, so to speak, a doubled self, may be understood as an expression of "5d: Phenomenological space."

As is especially evident in *Dialogue de l'ombre double*, the spatiality categories set out in this paper frequently span multiple categories within a single work. In this work, the implemented space of Category 1 directly corresponds to the self-doubling of Category 5d; the poetic framework of Category 5b reinforces this; and the "zigzag" corresponding to the diagrammatic logic of Category 4 constitutes the structural principle of the work as a whole — a mutual reinforcement among categories that characterizes the piece.

#### Stockhausen: Spatial movement through imagery

According to Matsudaira (2026), Stockhausen engaged with spatial music in the sense of Category 1 from early in his career, but a considerable number of works also involve "spatial movement through imagery." *Kurzwellen* was mentioned above; in *Telemusik* (1966), folk music from around the world produces a sense of "instantaneous teleportation to various places on earth," and in *Hymnen* (1966–67/69), national anthems from various countries create a similar effect. *Helikopter-Streichquartett* (1993), written as the third scene of *Mittwoch aus Licht*, actually places the string quartet members in four separate helicopters — yet since the live sound cannot be heard from inside the helicopters (a simulation of Category 1?), the work is only completed as music when the audio transmitted from each aircraft is mixed and heard in the concert hall, creating an imaginary spatiality through an extraordinarily convoluted situation.


## 7.

The taxonomy of spatiality categories proposed in this paper is provisional and may even be prone to category errors. The fact that only Takemitsu is cited as an example of Category 5c is a symptom of this.

Acoustic experience is spatial by nature. As Lucier's *I Am Sitting in a Room* (1969) highlighted (through electronic means), most of the sounds we hear in daily life — except when listening through headphones — are accompanied by reflections from space and already contain spatial information. An anechoic chamber, in eliminating reflected sound from space, might be described as a version of "outer space" that still has "air (and gravity)"; a cello performed in such a space would, for instance, sound entirely different from the same instrument played in a normal space (much as a cello fitted with a piezoelectric pickup would sound).

Yet the pitch and other information in sound does not change in quality even in an anechoic chamber. The claim examined earlier — that when speaking of sound we cannot escape spatial vocabulary — can be said to bear no relation to the fact that acoustic experience is spatial. What emerges from this is the hypothesis that we are ordinarily barely conscious of the general spatiality of acoustic experience, and that spatiality becomes foregrounded as experience only when a change or deviation from stable acoustic experience occurs.

It may be that attending closely to music actually excludes spatiality from music. Despite the fact that listening in a concert hall and listening on headphones to sound captured by a microphone and extracted from its space are qualitatively entirely different even acoustically, we accept this as sufficient — perhaps because the listener is interested only in the music and is indifferent to spatiality.

> [Supplementary note] Though distinct from spatiality, when listening to woodwind performance one notices, depending on one's manner of listening, that considerable key noise is produced. When concentrating on the music, such noise is pushed below the threshold of awareness — yet removing it entirely might sound unnatural in its own way.  
> The same applies to the production of electronic bass sounds. Low frequencies inherently sound slow to attack because of their long wavelengths. By adding an attack transient to serve as a timing "marker," the bass can be made to land at the right moment, and overall mix balance is thereby maintained.

Observing a piano keyboard, one can see that pitches are arranged from high (right) to low (left) as one moves from the player's right to left; yet one does not normally feel, when listening to a piano performance, that "the high notes are coming from the right." This is naturally the case for everyone except the performer — whether in a concert or in a recording, the spatial relationship between instrument, audience, and microphone does not produce such an impression auditorily either. Yet according to Matsudaira (2026), the most recent recording of Stockhausen's *Klavierstück* series employs an extreme mixing approach that positions the lowest notes at the far left and the highest at the far right, converting the wide registral leaps characteristic of the works into spatial movement between left and right. This is an example of a new spatiality discovered through unusual mixing that would not normally be undertaken.

The provisionality of the categories presented here is partly a matter of classificatory precision, but may also be evidence that spatiality in music has not yet been sufficiently examined. The inconsistencies in the above categories directly reflect the diversity of objects that the word "space" designates. Musical concepts such as pitch, dynamics, duration, and timbre each have clear definitions and methods of measurement; space does not. Moreover, space is so self-evidently present for us that we may not yet be accustomed to making spatiality itself a subject of inquiry.
